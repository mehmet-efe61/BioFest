Speaker

Onur Iyilikçi

Onur Iyilikçi

Neuroscience, Yale University

Onur İyilikçi specializes in a physiological and neurological aspect of social behavior and how it is coded in the brain. After his undergraduate and master’s degree Turkey, he decided to pursue a Ph.D. degree at Johns Hopkins University in brain sciences. During his time at Johns Hopkins University, Iyilikçi focused on elucidating how hypothalamic and mesolimbic systems interact to orchestrate information in the control of social rewards in conjunction with the physiological and environmental conditions.

Currently, Onur İyilikçi is an experienced researcher and a postdoctoral associate at Yale University. He is conducting research at Yale University on how social behavior is encoded in the brain. Brain mechanisms controlling social behavior and the dysregulation of these systems during early development is his primary interest as well as how these systems transform into adaptive social behaviors.

Iyılikçi’s talk will be about Developmental Social Neuroscience. In general outlines, the talk will cover the development of neuronal systems controlling social behaviors from a neuroethological perspective and research experiences in the field of Neuroscience.

Recommended Publications

  1. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867419304453

    The team uncovered important functional properties of hypothalamic Agrp neurons during mouse development. Hypothalamic Agrp neurons in the hypothalamus regulate food ingestion in adult mice. İyilikçi and his team studied the functions of Agrp neurons during breastfeeding using 10 days old mice. The study disclosed an offspring to-caregiver bonding facilitated by Agrp neurons. Agrp neurons are activated in response to nutrient deprivation in adult mice. The same response was investigated in neonatal mice. To measure Agrp neuron activation, one group was isolated from the nest to prevent milk ingestion. The study revealed that nest isolation but not milk (nutrient) deprivation activated Agrp neurons. Furthermore, the study showed that Agrp neurons in mice are blunted by non-nutritive suckling, an important component of maternal care, and is not further reduced by the availability of milk in the dam’s nipples. Chemogenetic overactivation of these neurons further increased the emission of these ultra-sonic vocalizations, but not milk ingestion.

  2. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5220225/

    İyilikçi and the team studied the sociosexual behavior in male Japanese quail, the findings underlined the important role of the medial preoptic nucleus (mPOM) on the mesolimbic reward circuitry promoting the activation of sexual behaviors in males. The medial preoptic area receives input from all sensory modalities and sends projections to motor pathways. The mesolimbic system promotes appetitive sexual activity and interconnectivity of mPOM and mesolimbic circuitry supports the hypothesis that sociosexual behavior is activated by the mPOM. The mPOM is bidirectionally connected to the ventral segmental area (VTA) in male quail. In addition, the lesions in mPOM disrupt pop-induced immediate early gene immunoreactivity which is associated with maternal behavior, however ipsilateral don’t have an effect on maternal behavior. To test their hypothesis on the regulation of sociosexual behavior by the medial preoptic area, the GABAa receptor antagonist was injected which deactivates the interest of the nucleus for a short time. The mPOM and VTA nuclei were of interest and whether their inactivation disrupted the appetitive sexual behavior in male quails was tested.

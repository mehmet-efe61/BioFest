Speaker

Elif Damla Arisan

Elif Damla Arısan

Bioinformatics, Gebze Technical University

Elif Damla Arisan is an experienced researcher on biotechnology. After obtaining her Bachelor’s degree at Marmara University, she worked as a visiting scientist at Johns Hopkins University and focused on cancer biology and polyamines. After she obtained her PhD in bioengineering at Sabancı University, she worked at Istanbul Kultur University as an associate professor and at Harvard Medical School’s Global Education Program on cancer biology and therapeutics. She, currently, is a full-time professor of biotechnology at Gebze Technical University. Her research interests include cancer biology, cellular death, cellular signaling pathways, mTOR and AMPK pathways that regulate cell growth and proliferation, metabolism regulation, and homeostasis respectively.

In addition to her work as a researcher, she is also a bioentrepreneur as she co-founded DAP Genomics. Under DAP Genomics, Arisan and her colleagues invented an innovative diagnostic kit that screens toxins and heavy metals while offering a descriptive analysis of genes contained in breast milk.

During the course of the COVID-19 pandemic, Arisan and her colleagues have also studied the virus in multiple perspectives. They studied the role of Peptidylarginine deiminases -a family of enzymes that have been connected to many inflammatory conditions- in COVID-19 based on computational analysis of BioProject transcriptome data, and investigated microRNAs -small, non-coding RNA molecules that function in post-translational regulation of gene expression- and their functions in COVID-19. They identified seven key microRNA-like sequences that highlight considerable differences during the comparison of five SARS-CoV-2 sequences to those from SARS, MERS, and two cold viruses.

Recommended Publications

  1. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31270852/

    Arisan and her team investigated autophagy, cell renewal by self-digestion, due to stress in endoplasmic reticulum (ER) in relation to atiprimod-mediated cell death mechanism in a rat cell line pituitary adenoma cells. Pituitary adenoma is the most common tumor type with a high recurrence rate because of hormone-dependent signal transducer and activator of transcription signaling. Atiprimod, a compound, blocks activation, and therefore, has anti-growth and anticarcinogenic effects in multiple carcinoma. In order to measure the efficiency of the therapeutic agent, its effects on ER stress-autophagy have to be researched.

  2. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23701543/

    Arisan and her team investigated the apoptotic efficiency of cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK) inhibitors in three prostate cancer cell lines that have different androgen receptor (AR) expression profiles. Targeting cancer by using cell cycle blocking agents is a promising strategy. Purvalanol and roscovitine are CDK inhibitors that cause the cells to go apoptosis in cancer cells. However, the co-existence of hormone-responsive and unresponsive cells due to the functional or non-functional AR the cells have is important as androgen signaling is critical in prostate cancer development and progression.
